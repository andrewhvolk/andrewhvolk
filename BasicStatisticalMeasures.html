Understanding Statistical Measures

A guide to measures of central tendency and distribution spreads

Sample Dataset:

4, 7, 10, 12, 12, 15, 18, 22

Measures of Central Tendency

Central tendency measures help us find the "center" or the most "typical" value in a dataset.

Mean (Average)

The sum of all values divided by the number of values.

μ = (∑x) / n

Example Calculation:

(4 + 7 + 10 + 12 + 12 + 15 + 18 + 22) ÷ 8 = 100 ÷ 8 = 12.5

Pros:

Uses all data points; mathematically useful for further calculations

Cons:

Sensitive to outliers; may not represent the "typical" value

Median

The middle value when all values are arranged in order.

Mdn = middle value (or average of two middle values)

Example Calculation:

4, 7, 10, 12, 12, 15, 18, 22

Median = (12 + 12) ÷ 2 = 12

Pros:

Not affected by outliers; better for skewed distributions

Cons:

Ignores the actual values of most data points

Mode

The value that appears most frequently in the dataset.

Mode = most common value(s)

Example Calculation:

4, 7, 10, 12, 12, 15, 18, 22

Mode = 12 (appears twice)

Pros:

Shows most frequent value; useful for categorical data

Cons:

May not exist or may be multiple values (multimodal)

When to use each measure: Use mean for normally distributed data, median for skewed data or data with outliers, and mode for categorical data or to find the most common value.

Measures of Spread (Dispersion)

Spread measures tell us how much the data points vary or are dispersed from the central value.

Range

The difference between the maximum and minimum values.

Range = Max - Min

Example Calculation:

4, 7, 10, 12, 12, 15, 18, 22

Range = 22 - 4 = 18

Pros:

Simple to calculate and understand

Cons:

Only uses two values; highly sensitive to outliers

Variance

The average of squared differences from the mean.

σ² = ∑(x - μ)² / n

Example Calculation (simplified):

For our dataset with mean = 12.5:

Sum of squared differences = 267

Variance = 267 ÷ 8 = 33.375

Pros:

Uses all data points; foundation for other statistical measures

Cons:

Not in the original units (squared units); harder to interpret

Standard Deviation

The square root of the variance; average distance from the mean.

σ = √σ²

Example Calculation:

Standard Deviation = √33.375 = 5.78

Pros:

In same units as original data; useful for normal distributions

Cons:

Still sensitive to outliers; assumes symmetric distribution

Interquartile Range (IQR)

The range of the middle 50% of the data.

IQR = Q₃ - Q₁

Example Calculation:

Q₁ (25th percentile) = 7.75

Q₃ (75th percentile) = 16.5

IQR = 16.5 - 7.75 = 8.75

Pros:

Robust against outliers; useful for skewed distributions

Cons:

Ignores 50% of the data (the tails)

Distribution Shapes

The shape of a distribution affects which measure of central tendency and spread is most appropriate.

Normal (Symmetric)

Mean = Median = Mode

Best measures: Mean, Standard Deviation

Right-Skewed

Mode < Median < Mean

Best measures: Median, IQR

Left-Skewed

Mean < Median < Mode

Best measures: Median, IQR

For normal distributions, mean and standard deviation are most useful. For skewed distributions, median and IQR are more representative.

Understanding these fundamental statistical measures is essential for data analysis and interpretation.
